“Long Form” and “Short Form” in Monstera: Disentangling Growth Habit, Leaf Morphology, and Ontogeny Through Morphological and Molecular Evidence

A taxonomic clarification and proposed multi-axis character framework · March 2026
Abstract

The informal designations “long form” and “short form” have become pervasive in the horticultural trade and collector communities to characterize morphological variation within the genus Monstera Adans. (Araceae, subfamily Monsteroideae). Despite their widespread adoption, these terms lack formal definition and, critically, conflate at least three independent axes of variation: growth habit (internode length, climbing versus creeping architecture), laminar morphology (leaf shape, fenestration pattern, length-to-width ratio), and ontogenetic stage (juvenile, transitional, and adult foliage). This paper examines the inadequacy of the binary “long form/short form” dichotomy through morphological character analysis of three case study species—Monstera deliciosa Liebm., Monstera adansonii Schott, and Monstera dubia (Kunth) Engl. & K. Krause—integrated with recent molecular phylogenetic evidence from studies of Monsteroideae. We demonstrate that morphological variants marketed under “form” designations frequently represent distinct ontogenetic stages of a single species, ecotypic responses to environmental gradients, or genuinely distinct taxa requiring formal description. We propose a multi-axis character matrix that decouples growth habit, laminar characters, and developmental trajectory as independent descriptors, offering a framework that accommodates natural variation without the taxonomic ambiguity inherent in current informal terminology. This framework has direct implications for accurate species identification, responsible horticultural labeling, and the conservation of wild Monstera populations increasingly threatened by collection pressure.

1. Introduction

The genus Monstera Adans. comprises approximately 50 accepted species of Neotropical hemiepiphytes and epiphytes in the family Araceae, subfamily Monsteroideae (Mayo et al., 1997; Zuluaga et al., 2019). Distributed from southern Mexico through Central America to southern Brazil and Bolivia, species of Monstera are characterized by their climbing or appressed-creeping habit, often heteroblastic leaf development, and—most conspicuously in the popular imagination—the production of fenestrate or pinnatifid adult laminae (Madison, 1977). The genus has long attracted scientific attention for its unusual developmental trajectories, including the sequential production of morphologically distinct juvenile, transitional, and adult foliage as individual plants ascend from the forest floor toward the canopy (Ray, 1990).

Beginning approximately in 2018, collector and horticultural interest in Monstera underwent a dramatic expansion, driven by social media platforms, online plant commerce, and broader trends in indoor gardening (Hinsley et al., 2023). This surge in popularity brought with it a proliferation of informal morphological terminology, most notably the designations “long form” and “short form,” applied across multiple species to distinguish what collectors perceive as discrete morphological variants. These terms have become entrenched in trade listings, online forums, and even some nursery catalogs, where they function as de facto infraspecific categories carrying significant commercial value—plants labeled as particular “forms” may command prices several-fold higher than otherwise identical conspecifics.

The central problem addressed in this paper is that the “long form” and “short form” binary, as currently deployed, conflates at least three biologically independent axes of variation. First, these terms are applied to differences in growth habit: internode elongation, stem diameter, and the ratio of climbing to creeping growth. Second, they describe variation in laminar morphology: leaf shape, size, the presence and pattern of fenestrations, and the length-to-width ratio of the blade. Third, and perhaps most problematically, they are frequently applied to specimens at different ontogenetic stages, such that a juvenile individual may be designated a different “form” than an adult of the same species and genotype. The collapsing of these three axes into a single binary term renders the terminology taxonomically incoherent and practically unreliable.

The consequences of this terminological imprecision are not merely academic. Misidentification arising from “form” designations has contributed to the mislabeling of commercially traded plants, complicating both provenance documentation and biosecurity screening (Vaglica et al., 2022). Where nominally distinct “forms” are in fact conspecific individuals at different developmental stages, consumers may be misled regarding the expected mature morphology of their acquisitions. Conversely, where “form” labels mask genuinely distinct taxa—whether described or undescribed—the conflation impedes recognition of species-level diversity with direct consequences for conservation prioritization, particularly given that several Monstera species occupy narrow endemic ranges in threatened Neotropical montane forests (Cedeño-Fonseca et al., 2020a).

This paper proposes to disentangle the “form” problem through a systematic examination of morphological variation in three case study species: Monstera deliciosa Liebm., Monstera adansonii Schott, and Monstera dubia (Kunth) Engl. & K. Krause. These species were selected because each exhibits pronounced heteroblasty, each is widely cultivated and subject to “form” designations in trade, and together they represent divergent positions within the molecular phylogeny of the genus. We integrate morphological character analysis with recent molecular phylogenetic evidence to propose a multi-axis character matrix that decouples growth habit, laminar morphology, and ontogenetic stage as independent descriptors. In doing so, we aim to provide a framework that bridges the gap between formal taxonomy and horticultural practice, enabling more precise communication about morphological variation without resorting to taxonomically misleading shorthand.

2. Taxonomic Background

2.1 Early descriptions and generic circumscription

The genus Monstera was first validly published by Michel Adanson in 1763, though the earliest species descriptions attributable to the group date to Plumier’s pre-Linnaean illustrations of Caribbean aroids. Heinrich Wilhelm Schott, in his extensive treatments of the Araceae during the 1830s and 1840s, substantially refined the generic concept, describing numerous species and establishing the diagnostic characters that would define Monstera for the next century: hemiepiphytic habit, distichous or spiral phyllotaxis, sympodial stems with conspicuous leaf scars, and inflorescences consisting of a spadix adnate to a deciduous spathe (Schott, 1832, 1860). Schott’s circumscription, however, was broad and somewhat heterogeneous, encompassing taxa later segregated into allied genera including Epipremnum, Rhaphidophora, and Stenospermation (Mayo et al., 1997).

2.2 Madison’s monograph

The foundational modern treatment of Monstera is the monograph of Madison (1977), which remains the most comprehensive single revision of the genus to date. Working primarily from herbarium material supplemented by field observations in Central America, Madison recognized approximately 30 species and proposed a sectional classification dividing the genus into two principal groups: section Monstera, containing the majority of species and characterized by perforate or pinnatifid adult laminae, and section Marcgraviopsis, a smaller assemblage distinguished by entire adult leaves and appressed-shingling juvenile growth. Madison’s treatment established critical diagnostic characters—fenestration pattern, petiole sheath morphology, spathe persistence, and seed structure—that continue to underpin species delimitation. Notably, Madison acknowledged extensive intraspecific morphological variation in several widespread species, including M. adansonii and M. deliciosa, but did not formalize infraspecific ranks to accommodate this variation, instead attributing it largely to environmental plasticity and developmental heteroblasty (Madison, 1977).

2.3 Croat and the expansion of species diversity

Thomas B. Croat, through decades of sustained fieldwork across the Neotropics, dramatically expanded the known species diversity of Monstera from the 1980s onward. Croat’s contributions include the description of numerous new species from poorly collected regions of Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Panama, as well as detailed treatments of regional floras in which Monstera features prominently (Croat, 1992; Croat & Grayum, 2020). Croat’s work emphasized vegetative diagnostic characters—stem cross-section, cataphyll persistence and fiber texture, trichosclereids in the lamina—that proved essential for distinguishing morphologically similar sympatric species in the field. His collections also revealed that species richness in the genus was substantially higher than Madison’s monograph suggested, a finding borne out by subsequent revisionary work.

2.4 Recent revisions: Cedeño-Fonseca and colleagues

The most significant recent contributions to Monstera taxonomy have emerged from the work of Cedeño-Fonseca, Croat, and collaborators, who between 2018 and 2023 described more than a dozen new species primarily from Costa Rica and Panama (Cedeño-Fonseca et al., 2018; Cedeño-Fonseca et al., 2020a, 2020b, 2022, 2023). These descriptions are notable for their integration of molecular data—typically plastid markers such as trnL-F and matK, and in some cases nuclear ribosomal ITS—alongside traditional morphological diagnosis. This body of work has revealed cryptic species diversity within complexes previously treated as single variable taxa. For example, populations formerly assigned to M. adansonii sensu lato in lower Central America have yielded multiple segregate species distinguishable by combinations of laminar fenestration pattern, cataphyll texture, and molecular divergence (Cedeño-Fonseca et al., 2020b). These findings are directly relevant to the “form” problem, as they suggest that some morphological variants circulating in trade under a single binomial with different “form” designations may in fact represent distinct species.

2.5 Molecular phylogenetics and subfamilial placement

Zuluaga et al. (2019) published the most comprehensive molecular phylogenetic analysis of subfamily Monsteroideae to date, employing both plastid and nuclear markers across a broad taxonomic sampling. Their results confirmed the monophyly of Monstera and supported its sister relationship to Rhaphidophora, a large paleotropical genus of morphologically convergent hemiepiphytes. Within Monstera, the molecular phylogeny recovered section Marcgraviopsis as monophyletic and nested within a paraphyletic section Monstera, a finding with implications for infrageneric classification. Tam et al. (2004), in an earlier molecular study, had similarly recovered Monstera as monophyletic but with limited species sampling. The phylogenetic framework established by Zuluaga and colleagues provides the essential comparative scaffold for interpreting morphological variation: characters that vary within molecular clades likely represent intraspecific plasticity or ontogenetic variation, whereas characters that covary with clade membership may signal species-level divergence.

2.6 Current state and the horticultural gap

As of 2026, approximately 50 species of Monstera are accepted in taxonomic databases (POWO, 2024; Tropicos, 2024), though ongoing fieldwork, particularly in the Chocó bioregion and Andean cloud forests, continues to yield undescribed taxa. The subgeneric classification remains provisional, with the two-section scheme of Madison (1977) requiring revision in light of molecular evidence. Crucially, however, the formal taxonomic literature has not engaged with the “form” terminology that has become standard in horticultural commerce. No published revision or flora treatment employs “long form” or “short form” as formal or informal descriptors. This gap between taxonomic practice and trade convention is the central motivation for the present study: the “form” lexicon fills a genuine communicative need—collectors do observe real morphological variation—but it does so with terms that are undefined, inconsistently applied, and biologically misleading. The framework proposed herein seeks to replace this informal binary with a structured, multi-axis character vocabulary grounded in the morphological and molecular evidence reviewed above.

3. The Three Axes of Form Variation

The commercial descriptors “long form” and “short form,” as applied to Monstera species in horticultural trade, function as composite labels that simultaneously encode assumptions about growth habit, leaf shape, and developmental stage. This conflation is not merely imprecise; it actively impedes both accurate identification and meaningful communication among growers, taxonomists, and conservation practitioners. In this section, we argue that morphological variation in Monstera is most productively understood along three independent axes—growth habit, leaf morphology, and ontogenetic stage—each governed by distinct developmental programs and environmental inputs, and each varying semi-independently of the others.

3a. Growth Habit: Vining versus Compact

The most immediately conspicuous distinction captured by the “long form” and “short form” labels is one of growth habit, operationalized principally through internode length. Plants described as “long form” exhibit elongated internodes, producing a scandent or trailing growth habit characteristic of actively climbing or light-seeking individuals. Those termed “short form” display compressed internodes, yielding a rosette-like, bushy, or compact architecture. This distinction is real and morphologically significant, but its utility as a classificatory character is severely limited by its plasticity.

Internode elongation in the Araceae is broadly understood as a shade-avoidance response mediated by auxin and gibberellin signaling pathways, analogous to the shade-avoidance syndrome well characterized in herbaceous angiosperms (Keuskamp et al., 2010). In epiphytic and hemiepiphytic aroids, internode length responds dynamically to light availability, substrate contact, and canopy position. A seedling of Monstera deliciosa germinating on the forest floor, for instance, produces elongated internodes during its initial skototropic phase—growing toward darkness, and thus toward the base of a potential host tree (Strong and Ray, 1975). Upon contacting a vertical substrate, internode length may decrease markedly as the plant transitions to an appressed climbing phase.

This environmental responsiveness means that a single genotype can manifest as either “long form” or “short form” depending on cultivation conditions. A Monstera adansonii grown under high light with a moss pole for support will typically produce shorter internodes and a more compact habit than a conspecific individual grown in lower light without vertical support. Ray (1987) documented this plasticity extensively in neotropical Araceae, noting that internode length in hemiepiphytic species is among the most environmentally labile vegetative characters. Zotz (2013) similarly emphasized that growth habit in epiphytes is best understood as a reaction norm rather than a fixed trait.

The implication for the “long form / short form” framework is straightforward: because internode length is a continuous, plastic character responsive to the growing environment, it cannot serve as a reliable basis for infraspecific classification. Growth habit is therefore necessary but insufficient as a diagnostic character, and the axis must be evaluated independently of leaf morphology and developmental stage.

3b. Leaf Morphology: Lamina Dimensions and Fenestration

The second axis of variation subsumed by informal “form” designations concerns the morphology of the leaf lamina itself, particularly its dimensions and fenestration pattern. In horticultural discourse—especially surrounding Monstera adansonii and its close relatives—plants are frequently described as “narrow form” or “wide form” based on the length-to-width ratio of the mature lamina. Additional characters of diagnostic relevance include fenestration type (circular to elliptical perforations confined within the lamina versus pinnate splits extending to the margin, or both), fenestration density and distribution, lamina texture (coriaceous versus membranaceous), and margin characteristics.

Fenestration is arguably the most taxonomically informative vegetative character in Monstera, and the genus derives both its scientific name and its popular recognition from this feature. The popular epithets “Swiss cheese plant” and “split-leaf philodendron”—the latter a misnomer that conflates Monstera with Thaumatophyllum—gesture toward a distinction in fenestration type that does correspond, imperfectly, to real morphological variation. Species such as M. adansonii and M. obliqua characteristically produce perforations (holes within the lamina that do not reach the margin), whereas mature leaves of M. deliciosa typically exhibit both perforations and pinnate splits. Madison (1977) treated fenestration patterns as taxonomically significant, and subsequent molecular phylogenetic work has broadly upheld the diagnostic value of fenestration type at the species level (Zuluaga et al., 2019).

Critically, fenestration characters appear to be under stronger genetic control than internode length. While the degree of fenestration varies with leaf size and ontogenetic stage, the type of fenestration is relatively stable within species across environments. A mature M. adansonii produces perforations rather than pinnate splits regardless of light regime, even as the density and size of those perforations may vary. This greater heritability makes fenestration pattern a more reliable classificatory character than growth habit, though it remains subject to the ontogenetic confound discussed in section 3c: juvenile leaves of fenestrated species are often entire, and the onset of fenestration varies with growing conditions and individual vigor (Madison, 1977; Ray, 1990).

3c. Ontogenetic Stage: Heteroblasty and the Juvenile–Mature Transition

The third and most confounding axis of variation is ontogenetic stage. Monstera, in common with many hemiepiphytic Araceae, exhibits pronounced heteroblasty—a dramatic, discontinuous change in leaf morphology across developmental stages that can render juvenile and mature individuals of the same species virtually unrecognizable as conspecifics (Ray, 1990). This phenomenon is sufficiently extreme in some taxa that juvenile forms have historically been described as separate species, a taxonomic error that persisted for decades in several cases (Madison, 1977).

The typical Monstera developmental sequence proceeds through several broadly recognizable phases. Seedlings produce small, entire (unfenestrated) leaves on slender stems. In many species, a subsequent “shingle phase” follows upon contact with a vertical substrate, during which leaves become distichous, appressed to the support surface, and markedly reduced in size—sometimes with overlapping laminae that create a tile-like pattern against the host trunk. This shingling morphology is so distinctive that species commonly encountered in this phase, such as Monstera dubia, are frequently sold to collectors without any indication that the mature plant will bear large, fenestrated, petiolate leaves (Madison, 1977).

As the plant ascends its host and transitions to the free-climbing juvenile phase, leaves increase in size, petioles lengthen, and fenestrations begin to appear. In M. deliciosa, juvenile leaves may be entire even at substantial size; perforations appear first, followed by pinnate splits as leaf size increases further. The full mature morphology—with deeply pinnate, multiply fenestrated leaves exceeding 60 cm in length—may not be expressed until the plant reaches the upper canopy, a process that can take years or decades in nature.

The implications for the “form” framework are profound. A juvenile M. deliciosa in cultivation—compact, with entire or sparsely perforated leaves—might be described as a “short form” plant, only to develop elongated internodes and deeply split leaves as it matures and is provided with a climbing support. The morphological transformation is not a change in “form” in any genotypic sense; it is the normal expression of a developmental program that the plant was always carrying. Conversely, a mature specimen maintained in suboptimal conditions may revert partially toward juvenile morphology, a phenomenon sometimes termed “retrogressive heteroblasty” (Zotz, 2013), further blurring the boundary between ontogenetic stage and environmental response.

Taken together, these three axes define a three-dimensional morphospace within which any individual Monstera plant occupies a specific position at a given moment in time. That position is determined jointly by genotype, environment, and developmental stage, and it shifts as any of these factors change. The informal labels “long form” and “short form” project this three-dimensional variation onto a single binary axis, collapsing distinctions that are essential for accurate identification, meaningful cultivar description, and scientifically grounded conservation assessment.

4. Case Studies

4a. Monstera deliciosa — “Large Form” vs. “Small Form” and the M. borsigiana Question

Perhaps no taxonomic question in the genus Monstera carries greater commercial consequence than the status of Monstera deliciosa var. borsigiana (Engl. & K. Krause) Engl. & K. Krause, described in 1908 as a smaller, more rapidly growing variety of the widely cultivated M. deliciosa Liebm. The variety was characterized principally by its more compact habit, shorter internodes relative to stem diameter, and somewhat smaller leaves at comparable developmental stages. However, Madison (1977), in his comprehensive revision of the genus, reduced var. borsigiana to synonymy under M. deliciosa, judging the distinguishing characters insufficient to warrant formal taxonomic recognition. Despite this synonymization—which has been followed by subsequent systematic treatments (Mayo et al., 1997; Croat, 1998)—the name M. borsigiana persists in horticultural commerce, where it has acquired a significance wholly disproportionate to its taxonomic standing.

The commercial stakes of this distinction have escalated dramatically with the rise of variegated cultivars, particularly ‘Thai Constellation’ and ‘Albo Variegata.’ In the ornamental plant trade, specimens marketed as “true M. deliciosa” or “large form” command prices severalfold—in some cases tenfold or greater—above those labelled “M. borsigiana” or “small form” (Hembrough, 2023). Sellers and collectors routinely invoke a suite of morphological characters to justify this separation, asserting that the two entities differ in internode length, mature leaf dimensions, fenestration pattern, growth rate, and—most conspicuously in online discourse—the morphology of the geniculum.

A critical examination of the morphological characters adduced to distinguish “large form” from “small form” reveals that none withstands scrutiny as a reliable diagnostic criterion. Internode length is notoriously plastic in hemiepiphytic aroids, varying with light intensity, support availability, and developmental phase (Ray, 1987; Zotz, 2013). Leaf size at maturity is similarly contingent upon environmental context. The fenestration pattern follows a well-documented ontogenetic trajectory in M. deliciosa (Sinha, 1999), and its expression at any given node is conditioned by the physiological status of the plant. No controlled study has demonstrated that fenestration pattern differs systematically between putative “forms” when environmental and ontogenetic variables are held constant.

The geniculum character merits particular attention because of its prominence in popular identification guides. It is frequently asserted that “true M. deliciosa” possesses a geniculum with distinctive ruffling or undulation at the petiole–lamina junction, whereas “borsigiana” displays a smooth geniculum. This character, however appealing in its apparent simplicity, lacks formal description in the taxonomic literature. Neither Engler and Krause (1908) nor Madison (1977) referenced geniculum morphology as a diagnostic feature, and no subsequent taxonomic treatment has validated it.

Molecular phylogenetic evidence offers no support for the recognition of var. borsigiana as a distinct evolutionary lineage. Zuluaga et al. (2019) recovered M. deliciosa as monophyletic but did not identify genetically divergent clusters corresponding to the “large form” / “small form” distinction. The available ITS and matK sequence data show minimal nucleotide divergence—well within the range expected for conspecific individuals (Tam et al., 2004). We recommend that variation marketed as “large form” versus “small form” be treated as an expression of phenotypic plasticity, ontogenetic stage, and cultivation practice rather than as a taxonomic distinction. Definitive resolution requires population-genomic approaches—ideally RADseq or target capture—across wild populations spanning the full geographic range of the species.

4b. Monstera adansonii — “Narrow Form” vs. “Wide Form” and Subspecific Variation

Among cultivated Monstera species, M. adansonii Schott is arguably the most widely grown and commercially available. Within the trade, a parallel nomenclatural system has emerged that subdivides the species into discrete morphological entities—most commonly the “narrow form” and “wide form”—that are marketed as distinct plants and frequently command different prices. A third designation, the “round form,” appears with less consistency.

Madison (1977) recognized two subspecies: M. adansonii subsp. adansonii, distributed primarily across South America, and M. adansonii subsp. laniata (Schott) Madison, occurring predominantly in Central America and northwestern South America. Madison distinguished the two on the basis of leaf texture (thinner in subsp. adansonii, coriaceous in subsp. laniata), lamina outline, and secondary venation patterning. More recently, Cedeño-Fonseca et al. (2020) elevated M. laniata to species rank on the basis of consistent morphological differentiation.

It is tempting to map the horticultural “narrow form” onto M. adansonii subsp. adansonii and the “wide form” onto subsp. laniata. The correspondence is suggestive but has not been formally tested. Phenotypic plasticity within M. adansonii is substantial—light intensity alone can significantly alter lamina dimensions—and intermediate morphologies are common in herbarium material from regions of subspecific overlap (Croat, 1992).

Molecular phylogenetic evidence bearing on the M. adansonii complex remains limited. Geographic structure has been detected, with Central American and South American populations showing differentiation at several plastid loci (Cedeño-Fonseca et al., 2020a), broadly corresponding to the subspecific distinction. However, the correlation with trade terminology is imperfect. We recommend that a formal molecular phylogeographic study be conducted, ideally including vouchered cultivated material sold under “form” designations. In the interim, authors and horticulturists should employ the subspecific epithets of Madison (1977) where geographic provenance is known, in preference to the informal “narrow” and “wide” terminology.

4c. Monstera dubia — Extreme Heteroblasty as a Confounding Factor

Few species illustrate the perils of morphology-based informal classification as vividly as M. dubia. This species undergoes one of the most dramatic heteroblastic transitions documented in the Araceae, a transformation so thoroughgoing that juvenile and mature individuals were historically treated as separate taxa (Madison, 1977).

In its juvenile phase, M. dubia exhibits the shingling growth habit characteristic of several aroid genera. Leaves are small (typically 3–8 cm), ovate to cordate, and lie flat against the host substrate in an imbricate arrangement. The adaxial surface displays a distinctive silvery-green variegation pattern. Internodes are extremely compressed, often less than 1 cm in length. Fenestration is entirely absent.

Upon reaching sufficient height on a vertical substrate—often 5–10 m or more in natural populations—M. dubia undergoes a striking metamorphosis. Mature leaves are large (30–45 cm), dark green, coriaceous, and deeply pinnately fenestrated, borne on a robust climbing stem with internodes of 5–15 cm (Madison, 1977; Croat, 1988). The silvery variegation disappears entirely. Observers encountering juvenile and mature specimens without the connecting stem have frequently failed to recognize them as conspecific.

This ontogenetic trajectory exposes the incoherence of the “long form / short form” nomenclature. A juvenile M. dubia would satisfy most criteria for a “short form” designation; a mature individual of the same clone would be classified as “long form.” The two descriptions refer not to distinct taxa but to obligate developmental stages of a single organism. In the horticultural trade, M. dubia is sold almost exclusively in its juvenile phase, as indoor environments rarely provide the vertical substrate height necessary to trigger the transition (Zotz, 2013).

M. dubia is exceptional in that virtually every macroscopic character—leaf size, shape, coloration, texture, orientation, internode length, and fenestration pattern—changes categorically rather than incrementally. The molecular phylogenetic position of M. dubia remains incompletely resolved; whether extreme heteroblasty represents a shared ancestral condition or convergent adaptation to the epiphytic niche is an open question tractable with expanded sampling.

5. Molecular Phylogenetic Evidence

The application of molecular phylogenetic methods to Monstera taxonomy has yielded findings that bear directly on the validity of informal “form” categories. The emerging consensus is that species-level boundaries are generally recoverable, but that infraspecific morphological variation—including the variation captured by “long form” and “short form” designations—rarely corresponds to phylogenetically distinct lineages.

The foundational molecular framework was established within broader phylogenetic treatments of Araceae. Tam et al. (2004) and Cusimano et al. (2011) resolved the position of Monstera using plastid markers, confirming the monophyly of the genus. Zuluaga et al. (2019) provided the most comprehensive phylogeny of Monsteroideae to date, employing plastid (matK, trnL-F, rbcL, trnK) and nuclear ribosomal (ITS) markers. Within the genus, Cedeño-Fonseca and collaborators demonstrated that molecular data could distinguish morphologically similar species confused under a single binomial—showing that phenotypic similarity does not reliably predict phylogenetic proximity.

The resolving power of available markers varies across taxonomic levels. Plastid markers matK and rbcL are effective at the genus level but frequently lack variation for infraspecific resolution. ITS provides finer resolution but is complicated by incomplete lineage sorting in recently diverged lineages (Alvarez and Wendel, 2003). No population-level microsatellite or SNP studies have been published for any Monstera species.

For the M. deliciosa complex, no molecular analysis has recovered a consistent genetic distinction corresponding to “borsigiana.” For the M. adansonii complex, geographic structure has been detected that broadly corresponds to the subspecific taxonomy, though the correlation with trade “forms” is imperfect. For M. dubia and other extreme heteroblasts, sampling remains too sparse to determine whether the developmental strategy is synapomorphic or convergent.

The overarching conclusion: informal “form” categories do not, in general, correspond to recoverable molecular lineages. They represent a heterogeneous mixture of ontogenetic stages, phenotypic plasticity, geographic ecotypes, and occasionally genuine infraspecific genetic structure, conflated under a single terminological system. The sole partial exception is the M. adansonii complex, where geographic subspecific structure appears to have both molecular and morphological support. Molecular phylogenetic sampling for Monstera remains heavily biased toward Mesoamerican species; South American diversity is severely underrepresented.

6. Proposed Clarification Framework

The preceding case studies demonstrate that the binary “long form / short form” terminology conflates at least three independent axes of morphological variation. We propose a multi-axis character matrix that replaces this binary with a descriptive system capable of distinguishing among these sources of variation. The fundamental principle is orthogonal description: each axis captures a distinct and independently variable aspect of plant morphology.

Axis 1 — Growth Habit. Characters include: mean internode length (mm, measured across five consecutive mature internodes), growth orientation (appressed/shingling, climbing/ascending, or free-standing/decumbent), and stem diameter at the midpoint of the most recent mature internode.

Axis 2 — Leaf Morphology. Characters include: lamina length-to-width ratio (L:W), fenestration type (none, perforate, pinnately split, or both), fenestration density (estimated as percentage of lamina area), and leaf texture (chartaceous, subcoriaceous, or coriaceous).

Axis 3 — Ontogenetic Stage. Three stages: juvenile (pre-fenestration), transitional (partial fenestration, intermediate size), and mature (full fenestration, reproductive competence attained or imminent). Estimated from node count or reproductive status.

Trade Designation Recommended Designation Axis Description
“M. adansonii narrow form” M. adansonii subsp. adansonii L:W > 2.5:1, perforate fenestration, mature phase
“M. adansonii wide form” M. adansonii subsp. laniata L:W < 2.0:1, perforate fenestration, mature phase
“M. deliciosa small form” / “M. borsigiana” M. deliciosa (compact internode morph) Varietal status unconfirmed; pending molecular evidence
“M. dubia short form” M. dubia (juvenile phase) Shingling habit, no fenestration, compressed internodes

We recommend that commercial vendors provide, at minimum, measurements corresponding to the three axes: internode length, lamina L:W ratio with fenestration type, and estimated ontogenetic stage. This framework is descriptive rather than explanatory and should be regarded as provisional, subject to revision as phylogenomic sampling improves. Nevertheless, it represents a substantial improvement over the current informal system.

7. Discussion

7.1 The Three Failure Modes

The case studies reveal three distinct failure modes of the “form” terminology. In M. deliciosa, the “large form” / “small form” distinction fails primarily because it conflates environmental plasticity with genetic identity. The morphological characters attributed to var. borsigiana are precisely the characters most responsive to cultivation conditions. The commercial incentive to maintain the distinction, driven by variegated cultivar prices, has created a situation in which market forces actively resist taxonomic correction.

In M. adansonii, the “narrow form” / “wide form” distinction partially succeeds—it captures real variation that may correspond to genuine subspecific divergence—but it obscures the taxonomic framework that already exists. Madison’s subspecific classification provides a formal vocabulary that is both more precise and more informative. The informal terms persist because collectors are largely unaware of the taxonomic literature.

In M. dubia, the terminology fails most dramatically because it conflates ontogenetic stage with taxonomic identity. The juvenile and mature phases are so morphologically distinct that applying “short form” and “long form” generates the absurd result of classifying a single plant as two different entities at different points in its life.

7.2 Implications for Conservation

Wild Monstera populations face increasing collection pressure as rare “forms” and species command high prices in international trade (Hinsley et al., 2023). Accurate species identification is a prerequisite for effective conservation assessment and legal protection under CITES and national legislation. When commercially desirable variants are mislabeled as distinct species—or when genuinely distinct species are collapsed under a single binomial—the resulting confusion impedes both conservation prioritization and enforcement. The proposed framework, by requiring explicit documentation of ontogenetic stage and environmental context, would provide a more reliable basis for assessing the conservation significance of wild-collected material.

7.3 The Path Forward

Resolution requires three parallel lines of investigation. First, population-level molecular phylogenetics employing reduced-representation genomic methods (RADseq, target capture, whole-genome resequencing) capable of detecting fine-scale genetic structure. Second, common-garden experiments in which accessions of putative “forms” are grown under standardized conditions to separate genetic from environmental contributions. Third, expanded taxonomic sampling in molecular phylogenies, particularly for South American species and taxa exhibiting extreme heteroblasty.

Until these investigations are completed, we recommend adoption of the multi-axis character framework as a practical interim measure. It does not resolve taxonomic questions but provides a structured vocabulary that prevents the conflation of independent sources of variation—a necessary first step toward clearer communication between taxonomists, horticulturists, conservationists, and the growing community of Monstera collectors.
